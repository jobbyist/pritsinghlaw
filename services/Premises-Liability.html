What Is Premises Liability?

Under California Civil Code § 1714, property owners must act with “ordinary care” to prevent harm to others on their property. The seminal case, Rowland v. Christian (1968) 69 Cal.2d 108, eliminated the old rules that treated guests and trespassers differently and established a general duty of care for all property owners.

But here’s the key: being responsible doesn’t mean being liable for every injury. We make sure the law is applied fairly, and we fight exaggerated claims head-on.

We defend landlords against a wide range of claims, including:

  • Slip-and-Fall Accidents: Wet floors, icy steps, or poorly maintained common areas.
  • Trip Hazards: Uneven pavement, broken tiles, or stairway defects.
  • Inadequate Lighting Claims: Tenants blaming you for accidents in dimly lit hallways or parking areas.
  • Alleged Building Code Violations: Claims that “unsafe conditions” caused an injury.
  • Elevator/Escalator Injuries: Accidents in multi-unit buildings or commercial spaces.
  • Negligent Security Claims: Assaults or thefts where tenants blame a lack of security cameras or locks.

The Legal Edge: Rowland Factors

The California Supreme Court in Rowland v. Christian outlined several factors that courts consider when deciding liability, such as foreseeability of harm, moral blame, and the burden of preventing harm. We use these factors in your favor—arguing that you acted as a reasonable property owner and that the alleged harm was not foreseeable.
