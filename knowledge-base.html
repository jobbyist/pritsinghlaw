California Real Estate Law Knowledge Center

Recent CEQA Reform Legislation

Governor Newsom signed AB 130 and SB 131 implementing sweeping CEQA reforms. AB 130 introduces exemptions for qualifying infill housing developments up to 20 acres, while SB 131 targets infrastructure projects. These changes represent the most significant housing policy reform in recent California history.

Key Provisions of AB 130:

  • Streamlined approval process for qualifying infill developments
  • Exemptions for projects up to 20 acres in designated areas
  • Enhanced transit-oriented development opportunities
  • Reduced environmental review timelines

The new legislation addresses California's housing crisis by removing environmental review barriers for qualifying developments. Projects must meet specific criteria including location within transit priority areas and adherence to local general plans.

Impact on Real Estate Development:

These reforms are expected to accelerate housing production statewide, particularly in urban areas where infill development is most needed. Developers should review their projects for potential exemption eligibility and adjust timelines accordingly.

Property Rights and Boundary Disputes

Boundary disputes are among the most common real estate litigation issues in California. Understanding property lines, easements, and adverse possession claims is crucial for property owners and developers.

Common Boundary Dispute Issues:

  • Survey discrepancies and conflicting property descriptions
  • Encroachment issues with neighboring properties
  • Easement disputes and access rights
  • Prescriptive easement claims

Early resolution through professional surveying and legal consultation can prevent costly litigation and preserve neighborhood relationships.

Landlord-Tenant Relations Under AB 1482

The Tenant Protection Act of 2019 (AB 1482) established statewide rent control and just cause eviction requirements, significantly changing landlord-tenant dynamics across California.

Rent Increase Limitations:

  • Annual rent increases capped at 5% plus inflation, not to exceed 10%
  • Applies to properties over 15 years old
  • Exemptions for single-family homes and condos (with conditions)

Just Cause Eviction Requirements:

Landlords must now provide specific legal grounds for eviction after a tenant has occupied the property for 12 months. This includes both "at-fault" and "no-fault" just causes, each with different notice requirements and potential relocation assistance obligations.

Environmental Compliance in Real Estate Transactions

California's stringent environmental regulations impact nearly every real estate transaction. From disclosure requirements to remediation obligations, environmental compliance is a critical consideration.

Key Environmental Disclosure Requirements:

  • Natural Hazard Disclosure Statement (NHD)
  • Environmental hazards including lead-based paint
  • Earthquake fault zone disclosures
  • Flood zone and fire hazard area notifications

Buyers should conduct thorough due diligence, including Phase I environmental assessments for commercial properties, to identify potential liabilities before closing.

Frequently Asked Questions

What qualifies as a boundary dispute in California? +

A boundary dispute typically involves disagreements about property lines, encroachments, easements, or access rights. These disputes often arise from survey discrepancies, unclear property descriptions, or conflicting interpretations of legal documents. Professional surveying and legal consultation are essential for resolution.

How do the new CEQA reforms affect my development project? +

AB 130 and SB 131 provide streamlined approval processes for qualifying infill developments and infrastructure projects. If your project meets the criteria—including location in transit priority areas and compliance with local general plans—you may be eligible for expedited environmental review or exemptions.

What are my rights under AB 1482 as a landlord? +

Under AB 1482, landlords can still increase rent annually up to 5% plus inflation (maximum 10%) for properties over 15 years old. After 12 months of tenancy, evictions require just cause—either at-fault (non-payment, lease violations) or no-fault (owner move-in, major renovations) reasons with proper notice.

What environmental disclosures are required in California real estate transactions? +

California requires extensive environmental disclosures including Natural Hazard Disclosure Statement (NHD), lead-based paint disclosures, earthquake fault zones, flood zones, and fire hazard areas. Commercial transactions typically require Phase I environmental assessments to identify potential contamination issues.
